Introduction


For many of us, it is easy to find both the objects in a picture and their boundaries. Humans are very good at identifying what something is and where it is given a clear line of sight, but what if we need to find the boundaries of objects in an enormously large number photos? While we could put a room full of toddlers with markers at work and tell them to outline the edges in a stack of photos, we can do better in both performance and morals by making a computer do this task for us! In fact, in computer vision, this is a general problem known as edge detection.

Formally, edge detection is the process of detecting the boundaries in an image that separate great changes in brightness or intensity. One of the reasons to do edge detection is that it is able to reduce a complex image down to a simpler one where only these edges are marked. Because of this, edge detection often acts as a preliminary step to other computer vision processes.




Before we dive into edge detection, we must talk about how an image is represented on a machine. While our toddler workforce may work with physical photographs, computers work with images as 2D grids known as pixel arrays. A color image, for instance, is commonly represented with 3 grids, storing the red, green, and blue color values at each pixel. With edge detection, since we are generally only interested in the intensity of a pixel, or how bright or dark it is, we will often first convert our color images to grayscale intensities which can be calculated from the red, green, and blue color values.
